# Findings

## Overview

This analysis models hospital food production and overproduction waste over an approximately **90-day period** (Dec 6, 2025 – Mar 5, 2026). Waste is estimated using a standardized tray model and scenario-based waste assumptions.

Results represent **modeled overproduction waste**, not observed plate waste, spoilage, or inventory loss.

---

## 1. Overall Scale of Modeled Waste

Under the **expected (10%) waste scenario**:

- **Total planned production:** ~41,043 lbs  
- **Estimated overproduction waste:** ~4,104 lbs  
- **Waste as % of planned production:** 10%

When normalized over the analysis window:

- **Average planned production:** ~456 lbs/day  
- **Average estimated waste:** ~45.6 lbs/day  

This corresponds to roughly **10–12 full trays worth of food per day**, which is consistent with standard production buffers in institutional food service.

---

## 2. Waste Distribution by Meal

Modeled waste is **evenly distributed across meal periods**, reflecting similar production volumes.

| Meal       | Planned (lbs) | Estimated Waste (lbs) | Waste % |
|------------|---------------|------------------------|---------|
| Breakfast  | 13,609.20     | 1,360.92               | 10.0%   |
| Lunch      | 14,109.98     | 1,411.00               | 10.0%   |
| Dinner     | 13,323.75     | 1,332.38               | 10.0%   |

No single meal period dominates overproduction waste, suggesting that **overall volume**, rather than meal timing, is the primary driver.

---

## 3. Day-to-Day Variability

Daily planned production ranged approximately from **425 lbs to 497 lbs**, with corresponding estimated waste between **42 lbs and 50 lbs per day**.

Key observations:
- Day-to-day variance is modest  
- No extreme outliers were observed  
- Waste scales predictably with production volume  

This consistency indicates that the production buffer behaves as intended—absorbing demand uncertainty rather than reacting to extreme fluctuations.

---

## 4. Interpretation and Boundaries

These results suggest that:
- Overproduction waste is **systemic**, not episodic  
- Waste magnitude is driven by **aggregate demand**, not specific meals or days  
- The model produces **plausible, stable estimates** under conservative assumptions  

Importantly:
- This analysis does **not** imply food is being unnecessarily wasted  
- It reflects the operational tradeoff between food availability and uncertainty  
- Observed waste at the plate or inventory level may differ materially  

---

## 5. What This Analysis Cannot See

The current data does **not** capture:
- Pre-production waste (spoilage, expired inventory)  
- Post-production waste (tray returns, portion mismatch)  
- Patient-level consumption or preference changes  
- Labor or workflow drivers  

As a result, this project should be viewed as a **baseline measurement of what is observable**, not a complete waste audit.

---

## 6. Implications for Future Measurement

This analysis highlights specific data gaps that limit deeper insight:
- Inventory tracking would enable pre-production waste analysis  
- Tray return logging would enable post-production waste estimation  
- Portion variance measurement would improve accuracy  
- Linking waste to census timing would refine forecasting  

These represent opportunities for **incremental instrumentation**, not wholesale system redesign.
